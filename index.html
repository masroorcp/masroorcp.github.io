Dark Energy Theories

Accelerated expansion of the late-time universe is one of the intriguing problems in cosmology. The simplest solution is to add a constant, known as cosmological constant (C.C.) to Einstein's theory of gravity, General Relativity (GR). Another possibility is to find a dynamical origin of the accelerated expansion by adding additional components (Dark Energy) to the theory of gravity. One can also try to modify the theory of gravity by carefully breaking the assumptions/principles behind the standard theory of gravity (Modified Gravity).

One of my research is to construct different dark energy theories or modified gravity theories to explain the accelerated expansion of the universe. This is a bit non-trivial, so one needs to be very careful to see if the model constructed is healthy. Along this line, I have constructed an Extended Minimally theory of Massive Gravity, and an interacting vector dark energy model, interacting scalar dark energy models, etc.

I am also looking for new models inspired by both observations and fundamental principles.

Testing Gravity

Until now, there has been a plethora of dark energy models and modified gravity models. The crowning glory of a theory is to pass the experimental/observational test. In the recent years, there has been a huge number of cosmological and astrophysical data (both from the electromagnetic wave window and gravitational wave window) available to test a theoretically healthy dark energy theory or modified gravity theory.

My second research interest is to confront different theories of gravity with cosmological data, such as cosmic microwave background radiation, and large-scale structure data. I have worked on putting constraints to various theories of gravity such as generalised proca theory, massive gravity, minimally modified gravity theories and so on. I am experienced in modifying Einstein-Boltzmann code (CLASS) to test these models.

In the coming years, there will be an enormous amount of data coming from EUCLID, and LSST with unprecedented accuracy and precision. I am looking to test theories of gravity at non-linear scales with the upcoming data.

Cosmological Tension

As the cosmological data becomes more and more accurate some discrepancies/mismatches in the estimation of crucial parameters of our universe assuming standard LCDM model. The most crucial such mismatches are in the estimation of the Hubble expansion rate today (H0), and amplitude of fluctuations (S8) from different observations. This is an additional crisis that cannot be ignored by the cosmologists today. If there are no systematics in the observations, then this issue invites us to discover new physics in the cosmos.

One of my research interests is to address the tensions in cosmology, particularly, the Hubble tension and the S8 tension. I have proposed late-time solutions to the Hubble tension using modified theories of gravity. I have also built new interacting dark sector models, in the context of scalar dark energy and vector dark energy, hoping that it can address both Hubble tension and S8 tension.

I am also interested in studying different models, that have potential to address cosmological tensions.

About me

I am a cosmologist interested in theories of gravity to understand the dark sector of the universe and testing gravity theories: from cosmology to strong gravity systems, like black holes. At present, I am a postdoc fellow at the Centre for Theoretical Physics and Natural Philosophy at Mahidol University, Thailand. Before joining this position, I held a short-term postdoc at Yukawa Institute for Theoretical Physics, Kyoto University, where I finished my thesis work on "Theoretical and Observational Constraints on the Cosmology of Theories of Gravity".

Useful
Tools

Here are some of the tools I use every day. Some of them are scientific tools, such as Mathematica, while others are more general tools that can help you manage projects and track your work time. This section will be updated as I become more familiar with each tool. My list will be updated if I discover any new tools that are useful.

  • xAct is a symbolic tensor algebra package written in Wolfram Language. This is useful in constructing Lagrangians from tensor objects and deriving covariant equations of motion. There are many other possibilities one can do with this package, for example, one can perform the Dirac algorithm and count degrees of freedom in complicated theories. With xCoba, one can also perform component calculations, given any metric for theory under consideration. There are many good examples one can find online, for example, click here. Here are some of my examples useful for cosmological perturbation.

  • The Cosmic Linear Anisotropy Solving System (CLASS) is an Einstein-Boltzmann code written in the C language. It calculates the evolution of cosmological linear perturbation and compares the observables from Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMB) and Large Scale Structure. Tools like this are highly important for the cosmological tests of theories of gravity. Using this, I have constrained different theories of gravity. The basics of cosmological perturbation theory are explained in the paper by Ma and Bershinger. To learn more about the approximation schemes, one can go through, the CLASS-II paper.

  • Superproductivity is a great tool for tracking how long you spend on your work. I like using this tool because it gives an idea of how much time I spend on my work. This helps me to limit myself to deviate from work. In addition, this application helped me understand how long it may take to complete a task. The best thing is that if you are disconnected from the task, you will be informed.

  • Trello is a working management tool. I use this tool to illustrate my project workflow. This gives a good idea of how each project progresses and what immediate steps should be taken next. You can find many interesting features for effective work management. With this tool, I learned how to organize my own projects.
